How Coupon Extensions Like Honey Steal Affiliate Commissions

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 7 min

If you use browser extensions like Honey (owned by PayPal), Capital One Shopping, or Rakuten, you might be unknowingly taking money from the content creators whose recommendations you trust. Here's how it works — and what you can do about it.

What Happens at Checkout

When you click an affiliate link from a review site, blog, or YouTube video, a tracking cookie is placed in your browser. This cookie tells the retailer "this creator sent you here" and ensures they get paid a small commission on your purchase.

But here's the problem: browser extensions like Honey overwrite that cookie at the very last moment, just as you're completing checkout.

The Last-Click Scam

Most affiliate programs use "last-click attribution" — whoever's link was clicked most recently gets 100% of the commission. Honey exploits this by triggering a pop-up like "Activate Rewards" at checkout. Even if no coupon is found, Honey's cookie is now the "last click." The content creator who drove you to the product earns nothing. Honey earns the full commission.

The Scale of the Problem

  • Honey has 17+ million users worldwide
  • PayPal acquired Honey for $4 billion in 2020
  • A class-action lawsuit alleges Honey systematically poaches commissions from creators
  • YouTuber MegaLag's investigation revealed Honey triggers commission-stealing pop-ups even when no discounts exist

Who Gets Hurt

Content Creators & Affiliates

Reviewers, bloggers, and YouTubers who research products, test them, and create honest recommendations lose their commission. The creator did all the work — writing the review, taking photos, testing the product — but Honey collects the payment.

You, the Reader

When creators can't earn from their work, they stop creating. The free reviews, comparisons, and guides you rely on become harder to find or get buried behind paywalls. The entire ecosystem of independent product research is threatened.

Merchants & Brands

Companies may end up paying double commissions — one to the original affiliate and one to Honey — or paying Honey for sales that would have happened anyway.

What You Can Do

3 Simple Steps to Support Creators

  1. Disable coupon extensions before clicking affiliate links. You can re-enable them after purchase.
  2. Click affiliate links directly — don't let extensions redirect you through their own tracking.
  3. Use creator-specific coupon codes when available. Commission is tied to the code, not the cookie.

How to Disable Extensions (30 Seconds)

  1. Click the puzzle piece icon in your browser toolbar
  2. Find Honey, Capital One Shopping, or similar extensions
  3. Click the toggle or "Remove" button
  4. Complete your purchase through the creator's link
  5. Re-enable the extension afterward if you want

Honey and similar extensions aren't "saving you money" — they're transferring money from creators to a $100 billion corporation. The coupon they apply is often one you could find yourself with a quick Google search. The difference is that without the extension, the creator who helped you make your decision gets paid for their work.

Support the independent creators you trust. Disable extensions, click directly, and keep free content alive.
