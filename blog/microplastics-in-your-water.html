Microplastics in Your Water: What the Latest Science Says

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 7 min

Microplastics — plastic fragments smaller than 5mm — are everywhere. They're in ocean water, tap water, bottled water, and even the air we breathe. But what does the science actually say about the health risks?

How Much Are We Consuming?

A 2024 study published in the journal Environmental Science & Technology estimated that the average person ingests approximately 5 grams of microplastic per week — roughly the weight of a credit card. Sources include:

  • Drinking water (both tap and bottled)
  • Food stored or heated in plastic containers
  • Seafood (especially shellfish)
  • Sea salt and beer
  • Airborne particles

Where Microplastics Have Been Found

The list of body tissues where microplastics have been detected grows every year:

  • Blood: A 2022 Dutch study found microplastics in 77% of blood samples tested
  • Lungs: Found in 11 of 13 lung tissue samples in a 2022 UK study
  • Placenta: Detected in human placental tissue for the first time in 2020
  • Breast milk: Found in 76% of samples in a 2022 Italian study
  • Brain tissue: Higher concentrations found in dementia patients (2024 study)

Health Effects Under Investigation

Research is still emerging, but concerning findings include:

  • Inflammation: Microplastics trigger inflammatory responses in tissues
  • Endocrine disruption: Plastic additives (BPA, phthalates) mimic hormones
  • Gut health: Microplastics alter gut microbiome composition
  • Cardiovascular risk: A 2024 study linked carotid artery microplastics to higher heart attack and stroke risk

Plastic Containers: A Major Source

When you microwave food in a plastic container, heat accelerates the release of billions of nanoplastics — particles even smaller than microplastics. A 2023 University of Nebraska study found that microwaving plastic baby food containers released over 2 billion nanoplastics per square centimeter.

Even "BPA-free" plastics shed microplastics. The replacements (BPS, BPF) show similar endocrine-disrupting properties in laboratory studies.

What You Can Do

  1. Switch to stainless steel or glass water bottles — eliminates a major daily exposure
  2. Use glass food storage containers — especially for reheating
  3. Never microwave plastic — even if labeled "microwave safe"
  4. Replace scratched/worn plastic containers — older containers shed more particles
  5. Filter your water — reverse osmosis and activated carbon filters remove microplastics

The Bottom Line

The science is clear: microplastics are in our bodies and the health effects are concerning. While you can't eliminate all exposure, switching to plastic-free food storage and water bottles dramatically reduces your intake from the sources you control.
